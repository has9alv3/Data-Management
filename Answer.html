Describing The Variable economic_status:

The variable economic_status follows a discrete uniform distribution with parameters a, b, and n [a,b integers with b ≥ a and n = b – a + 1]. Here, a = 1, b = 3, and n = 3.

The PMF of a uniform distribution is 1/n. For n = 3, the PMF of economic_status should be 1/3.

We find this TRUE because the three categories poor, middle, and rich all have a relative frequency of 33.33%. Both the mean and the median of economic_status are (a+b)/2 = 2, the middle category. From this information, we can also say that the skewness of economic_status is zero. The skewness is zero for all discrete uniform distributions.

#Q3

Required Variables:
  • CASEID (Case Identification)
  • BORD (Birth order number)
  • B11 (Preceding birth interval in months)
Steps:

1. Selecting required variables for this question and assigning them to q3_data.

q3_data <- bdhs_data %>% select(1,17,22)

2. Filtering q3_data by removing first birth and its twins. Adding NA where preceding birth interval, B11, equals to 999.

q3_data <- q3_data %>% filter(BORD != 1 & B11 != 0)
q3_data[q3_data==999] <- NA

3. As the preceding birth interval data is quantitative, suitable measure of central tendency is either mean or median.

4. Density curve with histogram will help us to to choose better measure of central tendency within mean and median.

q3_data %>% drop_na(B11) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = as.numeric(B11))) +
  geom_histogram(aes(y = ..density..),
                 binwidth = 7,
                 fill = "#84d6e9",
                 color = "#00b6e8") +
  geom_density(adjust=2,
               color = "#0294e8",
               lwd=1)+
  labs(title = "Histogram To Check Symmetry",
       subtitle = "(with density curve)",
       x = "Preceding Birth Interval",
       y = "Density") +
  theme_bw()

5. As it does not seem symmetric, suitable measure of central tendency is median.

median(q3_data$B11,na.rm = T)
## [1] 54
Interpretation:

For “preceding birth interval”, the median is 54 months (4.5 years). That informs us women tend to give birth at a 4.5 years interval.

The median is a suitable measure of central tendency for skewed distributions. Extreme values affect the median less than they affect the mean. Also, we can’t be sure if the extreme values are outliers or not. For example, the minimum value in “preceding birth interval” is seven months. Guinness World Records says the shortest birth interval (not including twins) in the world is around seven months. So there is a high possibility that this is an outlier. The maximum of our data is more than sixteen years, which does not represent our data at all. Extreme values like this can highly affect the mean. Therefore, the median is a better choice.

Required Variables:
  • CASEID (Case Identification)
  • V008 (Date of interview (CMC))
  • B3 (Date of birth (CMC))
  • B5 (Child is alive)
  • B7 (Age at death in months)
Steps:

1. Selecting required variables for this question and assigning them to q4_data.

q4_data <- bdhs_data %>% select(1,5,18,19,21)

2. Creating a quantitative variable age_of_child. The variable takes value equal to ‘current age’=V008-B3 of the children who are alive. Alternatively, it takes value equal to ‘age at death’=B7 for those children who had died before the interview date.

q4_data <- q4_data %>%
  mutate(age_of_child = if_else(
    (B5==1),
    V008-B3, B7))

3. Creating a dichotomous variable survival_status which takes value Survived if the child is alive and Died if the child is not alive.

q4_data <- q4_data %>%
  mutate(survival_status = if_else(
    (B5==1),
    "Survived", "Died"))

4. Let’s check the histogram of age_of_child (as it’s a quantitative variable, either mean or median is better measure of central tendency) for the children who are not alive.

q4_data[q4_data$B5==0,] %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = age_of_child)) +
  geom_histogram(aes(y = ..density..),
                 binwidth = 1,
                 fill = "#84d6e9",
                 color = "#00b6e8") +
  labs(title = "Histogram To Check Symmetry",
       subtitle = "(with density curve)",
       x = "Age Of Children Who Are Not Alive",
       y = "Density") +
  theme_bw()

5. It is highly skewed. So, we consider the median as a suitable measure of central tendency.

median(q4_data$age_of_child[q4_data$B5==0], na.rm = T)
## [1] 0
Describing Result:

Here, the median is 0 for a total of 357 cases. Sadly enough, that tells us the children in our observation died tended to live less than a month. They died in their neonatal period.

Our data ranged from 0 months to 48 months, but the median lies at the minimum. It shows us how skewed our distribution is. At least 50% of the children who died in our observation died in their neonatal period.

However, this does not mean the overall child mortality rate is high.
